From slavery to 35 hours De la esclavitud a 35 horas

Here is the time line of work week evolution

  • Unknown date - XVIII Century: In the XVIII century, Denmark and France abolished slavery in their territories. In the XIX century, Great Britain and the United States did the same, and many other countries followed suit. And although slavery is officially prohibited in almost all nations in the world today, it is estimated that millions of people are still living in slavery around the world.
  • XVIII Century y XIX Century - XX Century: The work week covered 7 days a week, in other words, every day. A total of 82 hours per week, which is almost 12 hours per day. and child labor predominated.
  • 1940: The 40-hour workweek is established in the USA. This means 8 hours a day with two days of rest per week.
  • XX Century: Most countries in Europe, as well as the USA and Canada, established a 40-hour workweek.
  • XX Century: The majority of Latin American countries have a 48-hour workweek, that is, 8 hours a day, with one day of weekly rest.
  • In the mid XX Century: The minimum working age is starting to increase, with the intention of reducing child labor.
  • 1980s: Germany is establishing a 35-hours workweek.
  • 2018: A company in New Zealand adopts a 28-hours work week, that is 7 hours a day with 3 days off. The company adopted this schedule after seeing an improvement in productivity.
  • 2022: More than 60 companies in the United Kingdom tested a four-day work week for six months. Once the test is finished, They decided to establish it after seeing that it improved the productivity of their employees.
  • 2023: Chile reduces its workweek from 45 to 40 hours.
